## Essential Questions:
1. What is the role of DesignOps on a design team?
DesignOps supports the design team by streamlining processes, managing tools, and ensuring consistency across projects. It removes friction so designers can focus more on design instead of operational work. Overall, it helps teams work more efficiently and at a larger scale.

2. Why is designOps important? 
DesignOps is important because design work becomes harder to manage as teams grow. Without it, teams can slow down due to poor processes, inconsistent workflows, and tool issues. DesignOps keeps teams aligned, efficient, and able to deliver work faster.

3. How are research operations critical to good design? 
ResearchOps makes user research more effective by organizing processes, tools, and participant management. This allows researchers to focus on insights instead of logistics. As a result, design decisions are more consistent, reliable, and grounded in real user data.

4. What are the four questions you should have answered by the end of a kickoff meeting?
- Why is the project important to the company?
- How will stakeholders determine if the project was a success?
- What approach or methodology will the project follow?
- What are the major dates or milestones, such as approvals from business stakeholders?

5. What is...
- The waterfall method? 
The waterfall methodology is a linear project approach where each phase must be completed and approved before moving to the next. Because it doesn’t easily allow changes once a phase is finished, it can cause delays when new requirements come up later. This makes the waterfall less flexible, especially for projects where things evolve over time

- The agile method? 
Agile methodologies focus on flexibility, collaboration, and releasing work in small, iterative cycles instead of following a strict linear process. Teams work closely together, rely less on heavy documentation, and use frequent releases and feedback to guide the next iteration. Common agile approaches include Scrum, Kanban, and SAFe, and many teams mix practices from different methods to fit their workflow.

- The double diamond?
The Double Diamond is a design framework that focuses on exploring and narrowing down ideas in two phases. Teams first diverge and converge to understand the problem, then diverge and converge again to develop the solution. This approach helps teams avoid jumping to solutions too early and supports better decision-making.

- lean UX?
Lean UX focuses on reducing waste by building and testing ideas quickly, especially in uncertain environments like startups. Teams create hypotheses, launch small MVPs, and use real feedback to learn and iterate fast. For designers, this means more sketching and collaboration and less time spent on heavy documentation. 

- dual-track product development? 
Dual-track product development runs discovery and delivery at the same time. The discovery track explores user needs and tests ideas, while the delivery track focuses on shipping features. This setup lets teams experiment without slowing down regular product releases.

- A design sprint?
Design sprints are a short, focused process—usually five days—used to solve a specific problem quickly. A small team brainstorms ideas, builds a prototype, and tests it with users all within the same week. Sprints are useful for deep dives and can be used within agile workflows or as a one-time activity.

## Reflection Questions
1. These chapters are really focused on organization and planning. Why are these critical to doing design in industry?
Basically, organization and planning matter in industry because design doesn’t happen by itself. I think without clear goals, timelines, and processes, good design work can easily get wasted or never shipped. Planning helps make sure the design actually lines up with business needs and gets delivered.

2. Which of the project planning and management methods appealed to you the most, and why? 
I think agile appealed to me the most because it lets teams adjust as they go instead of locking everything in upfront. I like that it focuses on collaboration, iteration, and learning from feedback. It just feels more realistic for how real products are built.

3. Between design ops, content ops, and research ops, which appealed to you most and why? 
I think DesignOps appealed to me the most because it basically helps designers focus on design instead of process headaches. I like the idea of improving workflows and consistency behind the scenes. It feels like a role that makes the whole team work better without being super flashy.
